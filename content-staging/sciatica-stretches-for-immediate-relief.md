## How These Stretches Work

Sciatica — pain radiating along the sciatic nerve from the lower back through the hips and down each leg — is caused when one of several structures compresses or irritates the nerve roots that form it. In roughly 90% of cases, a herniated lumbar disc is the culprit (Kreiner et al., 2014). Other common causes include piriformis syndrome (where a small muscle in the buttock spasms against the nerve), spinal stenosis (narrowing of the spinal canal), and spondylolisthesis (a vertebra slipping forward).

Each structure requires a slightly different approach. A disc herniation responds best to extension-based movements that help centralize disc material away from the nerve root. Piriformis syndrome responds to hip external rotator stretches and manual release. Spinal stenosis often responds to flexion-based stretches that open the spinal canal.

The 10 stretches below are organized by which mechanism they target. Work through them in order — they are arranged to progressively address the entire sciatic nerve pathway from lumbar spine to lower leg.

## Before You Start: Critical Safety Notes

Sciatica stretches are not a one-size-fits-all prescription. The same movement that relieves one person's pain can worsen another's. Keep these rules firm before, during, and after every session.

**The golden rule: Stretch to discomfort, never to pain.** A stretch should feel like a strong muscle pull — tension, pressure, maybe mild burning in the target muscle. If you feel shooting, electrical, or lightning-bolt pain down your leg, stop immediately. That is nerve pain, and pushing through it can set your recovery back by days or weeks.

**Never hold your breath.** Breathing steadily signals to your nervous system that you are safe. Shallow, held breathing increases muscle tension and can amplify pain perception. Inhale deeply through your nose as you settle into a stretch, exhale slowly through your mouth as you sink deeper.

**Start with the gentlest stretches.** If you are in an acute flare — the first 48-72 hours of a new or worsening episode — begin only with the supine knee-to-chest stretch and cat-cow. Add the more intensive stretches as your symptoms stabilize.

**Skip any stretch that peripheralizes your symptoms.** "Centralization" is when leg pain moves back toward your spine — that is a good sign. "Peripheralization" is when back or leg pain moves further down your leg — that is a warning sign. If a stretch moves your pain further from your spine, stop that stretch.

**Check with your doctor if:** you have had recent imaging showing a large disc herniation, you have osteoporosis, you experienced recent trauma (fall, car accident), or you have cauda equina symptoms (bladder/bowel changes, saddle numbness, rapid leg weakness). Stretches that involve extension are not appropriate for some conditions.

## The 10 Best Sciatica Stretches for Immediate Relief

### 1. Supine Knee-to-Chest Stretch

**Best for:** Disc-related sciatica, lumbar nerve root irritation, general lower back tension
**Time held:** 30-60 seconds per side
**Reps:** 2-3 per side, 2-3 times daily

This is the single most universally safe sciatica stretch. It gently flexes the lumbar spine in a controlled way, creating space between vertebral segments and reducing pressure on nerve roots. It also releases tight hip flexors, which are often overactive in people who sit for prolonged periods — a major driver of sciatica symptoms in office workers.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie flat on your back on a firm surface (floor or firm mattress). Place a pillow under your head for comfort.
2. Bend both knees and place your feet flat on the surface, hip-width apart.
3. Bring one knee toward your chest by gently gripping behind your thigh with both hands.
4. Keep your other foot flat on the surface and your lower back pressed into the floor.
5. Hold for 30-60 seconds. You should feel a gentle stretch in your lower back and glute, not sharp nerve pain.
6. Lower that leg and repeat on the other side.
7. For a more intensive version, bring both knees to your chest simultaneously and rock gently side to side.

**Pro tip:** Place a pillow between your knees if you have sacroiliac joint issues, and a rolled towel under the natural curve of your lower back if your spine feels hyperextended when lying flat.

![Supine knee-to-chest stretch demonstration showing a person lying on their back pulling one knee toward the chest while keeping the lower back pressed to the floor](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/piriformis-stretch.jpg)
*The supine knee-to-chest stretch creates lumbar flexion without loading the spine — making it safe for most acute sciatica cases.*

### 2. Figure-4 Piriformis Stretch

**Best for:** Piriformis syndrome, buttock pain, deep hip socket discomfort
**Time held:** 30-90 seconds per side
**Reps:** 2-3 per side, 2-3 times daily

The piriformis is a small pear-shaped muscle that runs from the sacrum to the top of the femur. In about one-third of people, the sciatic nerve passes directly through the piriformis rather than beneath it, making this muscle a primary source of sciatica-like pain even without disc involvement (Foster, 2010). The figure-4 stretch directly targets this muscle and is often the single most effective stretch for buttock-based sciatica.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor.
2. Cross your right ankle over your left knee, creating a figure-4 shape with your right leg.
3. Thread your hands through the gap between your left thigh and right shin, or place your hands behind your left thigh.
4. Gently pull your left thigh toward your chest. You should feel a deep stretch in your right buttock — this is your piriformis.
5. Keep your right foot flexed (toes pointing toward your face) to increase the piriformis engagement.
6. If your right knee pops or feels like it is pushing too hard against the floor, place a pillow or rolled blanket between your right knee and the floor.
7. Hold 30-90 seconds, breathing steadily. Do not bounce or force the stretch.

**Progression:** For a deeper stretch, add a slight internal rotation of the right hip — imagine your right foot sliding along the floor toward your left hip. This rotates the piriformis in a way that increases its stretch intensity.

![Figure-4 piriformis stretch showing correct positioning with ankle crossed over opposite knee and hands pulling the thigh toward the chest](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/exercises-to-avoid.jpg)
*The figure-4 stretch is the most targeted movement for piriformis-related sciatica, directly reducing compression on the sciatic nerve as it exits the pelvis.*

### 3. Cat-Cow Spinal Mobility Flow

**Best for:** General spinal stiffness, disc irritation, lumbar facet joint irritation, spondylolisthesis
**Duration:** 10-15 slow repetitions (1 rep = 1 cow + 1 cat)
**Reps:** 1-2 sets, 2-3 times daily

Cat-Cow is a gentle, rhythmic movement that alternates between lumbar flexion and extension. It increases circulation to spinal structures, reduces stiffness, and — in the extension phase — can help reduce pressure on nerve roots by opening the spinal canal. It is also deeply soothing to the nervous system, which makes it particularly useful in acute pain phases when anxiety and tension amplify the pain experience.

**How to do it:**

1. Start on your hands and knees, with wrists directly under shoulders and knees directly under hips.
2. Walk your hands slightly forward so your arms are perpendicular to your torso, and tuck your toes under.
3. **Cow position:** Drop your belly toward the floor, lift your chest and tailbone toward the ceiling, and look slightly upward. Your lower back makes a gentle concave curve.
4. **Cat position:** Round your back toward the ceiling like an angry cat — tuck your tailbone under, draw your belly button toward your spine, and drop your head. Your lower back makes a gentle convex curve.
5. Move slowly between the two positions, synchronizing your breath: inhale through your nose as you drop into Cow, exhale through your mouth as you round into Cat.
6. Perform 10-15 full cycles.

**Pro tip:** Keep your arms straight throughout — do not let your elbows bend or flare out. If your wrists hurt, place your fists on the floor instead of flat palms, or use push-up handles.

![Cat-cow exercise demonstration showing the two positions — cow with dropped belly and lifted tailbone, and cat with rounded spine and tucked tailbone](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/hero.jpg)
*Cat-Cow alternates between flexion and extension, making it one of the few stretches that can address both disc-related and stenosis-related sciatica.*

### 4. Glute Bridge Hold

**Best for:** Piriformis inhibition, glute amnesia, lower back stabilization, sacroiliac joint dysfunction
**Time held:** 30-60 seconds per hold
**Reps:** 3 sets of 3 holds, 1-2 times daily

Many sciatica patients have underactive, inhibited glute muscles — a phenomenon physical therapists call "glute amnesia." Prolonged sitting deactivates the gluteus maximus, forcing the piriformis, hamstrings, and lumbar paraspinal muscles to compensate during everyday movements like standing and walking. This overloads the piriformis and can compress the sciatic nerve.

The glute bridge builds back glute activation without loading the lumbar spine, making it one of the safest strengthening exercises for sciatica patients. The sustained hold version below also provides a mild stretch to the hip flexors, which are typically tight in sciatica patients who sit at a desk.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor roughly hip-width apart, arms resting by your sides.
2. Drive through your heels and squeeze your glutes to lift your hips off the floor until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
3. Hold the top position for 30-60 seconds. Keep your core lightly braced and your ribs from flaring upward.
4. Slowly lower back to the starting position with control.
5. Rest for 30 seconds between sets.

**Progression:** Perform single-leg glute bridges (extend one leg straight out) once the standard version feels easy. This increases the glute activation demand and addresses any side-to-side imbalances.

![Glute bridge exercise demonstrated in three phases: lying position, bridge raised, and top position hold showing proper hip alignment](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/exercises-to-avoid.jpg)
*The glute bridge addresses "glute amnesia" — a common contributor to sciatica where the primary hip extensors shut down due to prolonged sitting.*

### 5. Standing Hip Flexor Stretch

**Best for:** Psoas tightness, anterior pelvic tilt, disc-related sciatica, office workers with prolonged sitting
**Time held:** 30-60 seconds per side
**Reps:** 2-3 per side, 1-2 times daily

The psoas major is one of the most powerful hip flexors in the body, and it attaches directly to the lumbar vertebrae. When it becomes tight and short from prolonged sitting, it pulls anteriorly on the lumbar spine, increasing disc pressure and contributing to sciatica symptoms. Stretching the psoas can reduce this anterior pull and relieve pressure on the lower back.

**How to do it:**

1. Stand in a split stance — right foot forward, left foot back — with both feet pointing forward.
2. Bend your right knee slightly and shift your weight forward into the right leg.
3. Squeeze your left glute to tilt your pelvis under (posterior tilt) — this is critical, as it prevents you from simply arching your lower back instead of stretching the hip flexor.
4. Raise your left arm overhead for added feedback — if your back is arching rather than hips moving forward, you will feel it in your lower back.
5. You should feel the stretch in the front of your left hip and possibly the left lower back.
6. Hold 30-60 seconds per side.

**Pro tip:** Do not let your back arch aggressively. The posterior pelvic tilt is what makes this stretch effective for disc-related sciatica. If your back is arching, step your back foot further behind you until you can maintain a neutral spine.

![Standing hip flexor stretch showing correct split stance position with back foot grounded and pelvis in posterior tilt with arm overhead](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/sciatic-nerve-anatomy.jpg)
*A tight psoas muscle pulls anteriorly on the lumbar spine, increasing disc pressure — releasing it with this stretch can provide immediate sciatica relief for desk workers.*

### 6. Seated Spinal Stenosis Stretch

**Best for:** Lumbar spinal stenosis, central canal narrowing, pain that worsens with standing and walking
**Time held:** 30 seconds per cycle
**Reps:** 10-15 cycles, 2-3 times daily

Unlike disc-related sciatica, spinal stenosis pain typically worsens with extension (standing, walking) and improves with flexion (sitting, bending forward). This seated flexion stretch opens the spinal canal by reducing the extension forces that close it, providing relief for patients whose sciatica flares when they stand up straight.

**How to do it:**

1. Sit on a chair or bench with your feet flat on the floor, hip-width apart.
2. Place your hands on the back of your head or on your knees — avoid clasping your neck.
3. Slowly bend forward at the hips, bringing your chest toward your thighs. Round your upper back as you do so.
4. Allow your head and arms to hang forward naturally.
5. You should feel a gentle opening sensation in your lower back — this is the spinal canal widening.
6. Hold the forward bend for 5-10 seconds, then slowly return to an upright position by engaging your core and sitting up tall.
7. Repeat for 10-15 cycles.

**Caution:** If you have a forward-bending disc herniation, this stretch may not be appropriate. Listen to your body — if the forward bend increases your leg symptoms, switch to the McKenzie extension stretches instead.

![Seated forward flexion stretch demonstrated showing correct seated posture with rounded back and hands resting behind head](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/piriformis-stretch.jpg)
*Seated flexion opens the spinal canal — the key mechanism for stenosis-related sciatica that worsens with standing and walking.*

### 7. Cobra Pose (Prone Extension)

**Best for:** Disc material centralization, lumbar nerve root decompression, extension-sensitive stenosis
**Time held:** 10-30 seconds per hold
**Reps:** 8-10 repetitions, holding each extension for 10-30 seconds

The cobra pose is an extension-based stretch based on the McKenzie Method's directional preference principles. When disc material is displaced posteriorly (pushing toward the nerve root), extension-based movements can help push that material back toward its natural position — a process called "centralization." Centralization is a strong positive prognostic sign in sciatica rehabilitation.

This is one of the most studied and evidence-supported stretches for disc-related sciatica. The 2012 RCT by Petersen et al. demonstrated that McKenzie-based extension protocols produced significantly greater pain reduction than standard physiotherapy protocols in disc herniation patients.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie face down (prone) on the floor with your legs extended straight back, feet together, toes pointing behind you.
2. Place your palms flat on the floor directly under your shoulders, elbows bent and tucked close to your body.
3. Keeping your hips and legs on the floor, press through your palms to straighten your arms and lift your chest off the floor.
4. Only lift as high as you can while keeping your hips grounded — do not push up as high as you can.
5. Look straight down at the floor (do not tilt your head back) to keep your neck neutral.
6. Hold for 10-30 seconds. You should feel a mild extension pressure in your lower back — not sharp nerve pain.
7. Lower back down slowly. Rest for 5-10 seconds between reps.

**If the full cobra is too intense:** Start with the "sphinx" variation — lift only onto your forearms with elbows under shoulders. This provides a gentler extension that is often better tolerated in acute phases.

![Cobra pose demonstrated showing a person lying prone with palms under shoulders pressing up to extend the lumbar spine while hips remain on the floor](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/hero.jpg)
*The cobra pose uses spinal extension to centralize herniated disc material away from the nerve root — supported by McKenzie Method research.*

### 8. Nerve Glide / Sciatic Nerve Flossing

**Best for:** Sciatic nerve irritation, nerve root tension, chronic sciatica with pins and needles or tingling
**Time held:** 3-5 seconds per position in the cycle
**Reps:** 10-15 cycles per side, 2 times daily

Unlike muscle stretches, which target connective tissue, nerve glides target the sciatic nerve itself. A nerve glide (sometimes called nerve flossing) gently moves the nerve through its surrounding sheath, preventing adhesions and reducing sensitivity. Think of it like working a kink out of a garden hose — the movement helps restore normal sliding between the nerve and adjacent tissues.

**Critically:** nerve glides are not aggressive stretches. The movements should be small, smooth, and pain-free. If you feel shooting pain during a nerve glide, reduce your range of motion significantly.

**How to do it:**

1. Sit on a chair with your feet flat on the floor, knees at roughly 90 degrees.
2. Extend your right leg straight out in front of you, flexing your ankle (toes pulled toward your shin).
3. Simultaneously look up at the ceiling and straighten your knee fully.
4. Then bend your knee and tuck your chin (look down at your lap) simultaneously.
5. This is one cycle. The alternating tension and release "flosses" the nerve.
6. Perform 10-15 slow cycles per side, 2 times daily.

**Pro tip:** Perform this stretch in the morning before any other activity — nerve tissues are less irritable when you first wake up, and warming up your nerve mobility first can make subsequent stretching more effective.

![Nerve glide exercise showing seated position with leg extended, ankle flexed, and head looking up during the nerve tension phase of the floss](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/sciatic-nerve-anatomy.jpg)
*Nerve glides work the sciatic nerve through its sheath to prevent adhesions and reduce nerve sensitivity — gentle and smooth, never aggressive.*

### 9. Side-Lying Piriformis Release with Props

**Best for:** Deep piriformis trigger points, unilateral buttock pain, piriformis syndrome resistant to other stretches
**Time held:** 60-90 seconds per side
**Reps:** 1-2 passes per side, 1 time daily

If the figure-4 stretch is not providing sufficient piriformis relief, adding a targeted self-release using a ball or foam roller can reach the deeper portions of the muscle. This version requires a lacrosse ball or tennis ball and targets the piriformis at its most common trigger point — approximately one-third of the way between the top of the greater trochanter (bony point on the side of the hip) and the sacrum.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie on your right side with your right hip on a yoga mat or carpeted floor. Stack your legs or place your top leg slightly in front of the bottom leg for stability.
2. Place a lacrosse ball (or tennis ball for a gentler version) under the outer aspect of your right buttock — feel for the prominent bony part of your pelvis (greater trochanter). The piriformis trigger point is roughly one to two inches toward your sacrum from that bony landmark.
3. Slowly roll your body weight onto the ball. You are looking for a tender, tight spot — not pain radiating down your leg.
4. Once you find a tender spot, hold still and breathe deeply. Let the tissue slowly release — this takes 60-90 seconds of sustained pressure, not a quick roll.
5. Roll slowly between areas, spending at least 60 seconds on each tender zone. Cover the full outer buttock area in the piriformis region.
6. After you finish the right side, flip to the left side and repeat.

**Caution:** Stop immediately if you feel sharp nerve-type shooting pain radiating down your leg. Some localized buttock pain during the release is expected; radicular pain is not.

![Side-lying piriformis release using a lacrosse ball positioned correctly between the greater trochanter and sacrum on the outer buttock](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/exercises-to-avoid.jpg)
*Deep piriformis self-release with a lacrosse ball targets the muscle at its most common trigger point — approximately one-third of the way from the hip bone toward the tailbone.*

### 10. Press-Up (McKenzie Extension)

**Best for:** Disc herniation, central canal stenosis, patients who centralize with extension, severe acute sciatica when even gentle movement is difficult
**Time held:** 10-30 seconds per hold
**Reps:** 8-12 reps, 3-4 times daily

The press-up is the McKenzie Method's flagship exercise for lumbar disc herniation. Unlike the cobra pose (which uses some upper body muscular effort), the press-up uses the arms to generate the extension force, which allows for more consistent, controlled extension even in severe pain. It is also easier to modify — you can reduce the range by only partially straightening your arms.

This exercise is so well-supported by evidence that physical therapists often teach it before any other exercise for acute disc-related sciatica. A 2013 systematic review by Halliday et al. found that extension-based protocols were among the most effective conservative interventions for lumbar disc herniation with radiculopathy.

**How to do it:**

1. Lie face down (prone) on the floor, arms bent, palms flat on the floor near your shoulders.
2. Using your arms alone (not engaging your back muscles), straighten your arms to press your upper body up. Keep your hips and legs relaxed and on the floor. Do not engage your back — let your arms do the work.
3. Your lower back will naturally arch into extension as your chest lifts.
4. For the gentler version: only straighten your arms half-way and hold. For the more intensive version: straighten fully and let your pelvis lift slightly off the floor.
5. Look downward to keep your neck neutral. Do not arch your neck aggressively.
6. Hold 10-30 seconds at the top, then lower back down with control.
7. Rest 5-10 seconds between reps.

**If symptoms peripheralize (pain moves further down the leg):** Stop immediately. This may indicate that extension-based exercises are not appropriate for your specific disc pathology.

![Press-up exercise demonstrating correct prone position with arms straight pressing torso upward while hips remain on the floor](/images/sciatica-exercises-to-avoid/hero.jpg)
*The McKenzie press-up uses arm strength for controlled extension — making it the safest and most consistent extension option for acute disc-related sciatica.*

## Stretching Schedule for Acute Sciatica Flares

The most effective stretching approach is not random — it is structured around your pain phase. Here is a three-phase protocol based on evidence-based rehabilitation principles.

### Phase 1: Acute Flare (Days 1-3)

Pain is at its peak. Your goal is gentle nervous system calming and preventing further irritation.

**Morning session (10 minutes):**
- Cat-Cow: 10 cycles
- Supine Knee-to-Chest (both knees): 3 × 30 seconds
- Nerve Glide: 10 cycles per side

**Midday session (8 minutes):**
- Supine Knee-to-Chest (single leg): 2 × 30 seconds per side
- Figure-4 Stretch (gentle): 2 × 30 seconds per side

**Evening session (10 minutes):**
- Cat-Cow: 10 cycles
- Seated Spinal Stenosis Stretch (gentle forward bend): 10 cycles
- Nerve Glide: 10 cycles per side

### Phase 2: Subacute (Days 4-14)

Pain is stabilizing. Your goal is to progressively load the stretches and add strengthening components.

Add to the above schedule:
- Cobra Pose: 8 × 15-second holds (morning and evening)
- Glute Bridge Hold: 3 × 30-second holds (once daily)
- Standing Hip Flexor: 2 × 45 seconds per side (once daily, if hip flexor tightness is a concern)

### Phase 3: Recovery Maintenance (Weeks 3-8+)

Pain is significantly improved. Your goal is building resilience and preventing recurrence.

Rotate through all 10 stretches in the following order:
1. Supine Knee-to-Chest
2. Figure-4 Piriformis
3. Cat-Cow (5 cycles as warm-up)
4. Glute Bridge (3 holds, progressing to single-leg)
5. Standing Hip Flexor
6. Cobra Pose or McKenzie Press-Up

## When Stretches Are Not Enough

Sciatica stretches are a powerful tool in your recovery, but they are not a cure. If any of the following apply to you, stretches alone will not resolve the issue — professional evaluation is required.

**Red flags requiring immediate medical attention:**
- Loss of bowel or bladder control (possible cauda equina syndrome — surgical emergency)
- Progressive weakness — foot drop that is getting worse, inability to stand on your toes, numbness spreading into both legs
- Numbness in the saddle area (inner thighs, groin, perineum)
- Severe, unrelenting pain that does not change with any position for more than 72 hours
- Symptoms started after a major fall, car accident, or sports collision

**Stretches alone are insufficient when:**
- Your symptoms have not improved after 6 weeks of consistent stretching and conservative management
- Your pain is caused by a large central disc herniation visible on MRI — some herniations do not respond to extension-based stretches and may require surgical consultation
- You have spinal stenosis severe enough that even gentle flexion-based stretches are not providing relief
- You have had recurrent sciatica episodes (3+ per year) — at that point, you need a structured physical therapy program, not just stretches

**What to ask your physical therapist:** Request a McKenzie-certified or credentialed mechanical diagnosis and therapy (MDT) practitioner. The McKenzie assessment uses repeated movements to identify your specific "directional preference" — whether extension, flexion, or lateral shifts reduce your symptoms — which allows for a far more targeted exercise prescription than a generic stretching program.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What stretches immediately relieve sciatica pain?

The piriformis stretch, supine knee-to-chest stretch, cat-cow mobility flow, and seated spinal stenosis stretch are among the fastest-acting stretches for sciatica relief. These target the most common compression points along the sciatic nerve pathway. Most people feel measurable relief within 5-10 minutes of correctly performing these stretches in sequence.

### How often should I stretch for sciatica?

Most physical therapists recommend 2-3 sessions per day during an acute flare, with each session lasting 10-15 minutes. Perform stretches gently — never push through sharp, shooting pain. Consistency matters more than intensity. After symptoms stabilize (usually 2-4 weeks), you can reduce to once daily as a maintenance practice.

### Can stretching make sciatica worse?

Yes, if you perform the wrong type of stretch. Forward bends like standing toe touches and seated hamstring stretches can increase intradiscal pressure and tension the sciatic nerve, worsening symptoms. Stick to neutral-spine and extension-based stretches. If any stretch peripheralizes your leg pain (moves it further down your leg), stop that stretch immediately.

### How long does it take for sciatica stretches to work?

Many people feel measurable relief within 5-10 minutes of correctly performing the stretches in this guide. For lasting improvement, expect 2-6 weeks of consistent practice. Chronic or severe cases may take longer. Stretching is a management tool, not a cure — you will likely need to maintain the practice long-term if your underlying condition (disc herniation, stenosis) is permanent.

### What is the best stretch for piriformis syndrome?

The figure-4 piriformis stretch — where you lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, and gently pull the bottom leg toward your chest — directly targets the piriformis muscle. Hold for 30-60 seconds per side and repeat 2-3 times. A lacrosse ball self-release (stretch #9) is also highly effective for the deep portion of the piriformis that the figure-4 stretch cannot fully reach.

### Should I use heat or ice before stretching?

Heat for 10-15 minutes before stretching relaxes tight muscles and increases blood flow, making stretches more effective. Ice after stretching only if you experience any increased soreness after the session. During an acute flare with significant inflammation, start with ice only and add heat once inflammation subsides. Never apply ice directly to bare skin — always use a cloth barrier.
