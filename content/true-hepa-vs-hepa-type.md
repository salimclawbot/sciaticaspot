# True HEPA vs HEPA-Type: What's the Difference? (2026)

**By Dr. Alex Chen** · Last updated March 10, 2026

**True HEPA filters capture 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns — the hardest particle size to trap. HEPA-type filters have no standardized efficiency and typically capture only 85–95%.** That 5–15% gap means millions of allergens, mold spores, and fine particles pass through on every air cycle. For allergies, asthma, or any respiratory concern, True HEPA is the only grade worth buying.

The air purifier market has a labeling problem. Walk down the air purifier aisle — or scroll through Amazon listings — and you will see "HEPA" on virtually every box. HEPA filter. HEPA-type filter. HEPA-style filter. HEPA-like filter. True HEPA filter. Medical-grade HEPA. Hospital-grade HEPA. H13 HEPA.

Some of these terms mean something specific and verifiable. Others are marketing language designed to imply performance that the product does not deliver. The difference between a True HEPA filter and a "HEPA-type" filter is not a subtle branding distinction — it is a measurable performance gap that determines whether your air purifier actually removes the particles making you sick.

This guide explains exactly what each HEPA label means, how the grading system works, why the efficiency gap compounds over time, and how to verify claims before spending your money.

## Why This Distinction Matters

A True HEPA filter captures **99.97%** of particles at 0.3 microns. A HEPA-type filter might capture **90%** — or 85%, or 95%. There is no standard, so you do not know.

That sounds like a small gap. It is not.

At 99.97% efficiency, 3 out of every 10,000 particles pass through the filter. At 90% efficiency, 1,000 out of every 10,000 pass through — **333 times more leakage**. Run both purifiers continuously in a bedroom for 8 hours while you sleep, and the True HEPA room has dramatically lower particle concentrations than the HEPA-type room.

For someone with dust mite allergies, pet dander sensitivity, mold reactions, or asthma, that difference is not theoretical. It is the difference between waking up congested or breathing clearly. For anyone investing $100–300 in an air purifier specifically to improve respiratory health, buying a HEPA-type filter undermines the entire purpose.

## What Is True HEPA?

True HEPA is a specific, testable performance standard — not a marketing term. It has two authoritative definitions:

### US Definition (Department of Energy)

The US DOE defines a HEPA filter as one that captures **at least 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns** in diameter. This standard was originally developed for nuclear applications in the 1940s to capture radioactive particles, and it remains the benchmark for high-efficiency particulate filtration.

**Why 0.3 microns?** This is the **most penetrating particle size (MPPS)** — the particle diameter that is hardest for filter fibers to capture. Smaller particles are caught by diffusion (they bounce around randomly and stick to fibers). Larger particles are caught by interception and impaction (they are too big to follow the airstream around fibers). Particles at 0.3 microns are too large for efficient diffusion capture and too small for efficient interception — they occupy the worst-case gap between the two mechanisms.

This means a filter tested at 0.3 microns is actually more efficient at both smaller and larger particle sizes. A True HEPA filter does not capture "particles down to 0.3 microns" — it captures particles at the size where it performs worst, meaning it performs even better at all other sizes.

### European Definition (EN 1822)

The European standard EN 1822 classifies HEPA filters by grade from H10 through H14 (and beyond into ULPA territory). The two grades that correspond to "True HEPA" performance are:

- **H13:** ≥99.95% efficiency at MPPS
- **H14:** ≥99.995% efficiency at MPPS

The EN 1822 test protocol is slightly more rigorous than the US DOE method because it tests at the MPPS specifically (which can be near 0.1–0.2 microns depending on the filter) rather than at a fixed 0.3 microns. An H13 filter tested under EN 1822 meets or exceeds the US True HEPA standard.

### The Bottom Line

When a product is labeled "True HEPA," it should meet the US DOE standard (99.97% at 0.3 microns) or the equivalent H13/H14 EN 1822 grade. This is a real, testable, independently verifiable claim. If it meets this threshold, it works. If it does not, it is not True HEPA — regardless of what the box says.

## What Is HEPA-Type?

"HEPA-type" is a marketing term with **no standardized definition**. There is no regulatory body that defines what "HEPA-type" means, no testing protocol it must meet, and no minimum efficiency it must demonstrate.

In practice, HEPA-type filters are lower-grade filter media — typically corresponding to H10, H11, or H12 under the EN 1822 classification — that capture somewhere between 85% and 99% of particles. The exact efficiency is often unspecified by the manufacturer, which is itself a red flag.

### Why HEPA-Type Exists

Two reasons: cost and airflow.

**Cost:** True HEPA filter media requires extremely dense, precisely manufactured glass fiber. The denser the fiber mesh, the more expensive the raw material and the tighter the manufacturing tolerances. HEPA-type media uses looser fiber that costs less to produce.

**Airflow resistance:** Denser filter media creates more resistance to airflow (measured as pressure drop). Higher resistance means the purifier needs a more powerful — and more expensive — fan motor to push air through the filter while maintaining adequate CADR. A HEPA-type filter allows more airflow with a cheaper motor, enabling a lower retail price.

The result: HEPA-type purifiers can be sold for $30–80 while True HEPA purifiers start at $100+. The price difference attracts budget buyers who see "HEPA" on the box and assume equivalent performance.

### Other Non-Standard Labels

"HEPA-type" is the most common vague label, but manufacturers use several others:

| Label | What It Means | Standardized? |
|-------|-------------|:-------------:|
| **True HEPA** | 99.97% at 0.3 microns (US DOE) | ✅ Yes |
| **H13 HEPA** | 99.95% at MPPS (EN 1822) | ✅ Yes |
| **H14 HEPA** | 99.995% at MPPS (EN 1822) | ✅ Yes |
| **Medical-grade HEPA** | Typically H13 or H14, but not a formal standard | ⚠️ Informal |
| **Hospital-grade HEPA** | Same as medical-grade — marketing, not a classification | ⚠️ Informal |
| **HEPA-type** | Unknown efficiency, no standard | ❌ No |
| **HEPA-style** | Same as HEPA-type — no standard | ❌ No |
| **HEPA-like** | Same — no standard | ❌ No |
| **99% HEPA** | Claims 99% but does not specify particle size or test method | ❌ No |

The rule is simple: if the label does not say **True HEPA**, **H13**, or **H14** with a specific efficiency percentage at a specific particle size, treat it as HEPA-type and assume lower performance.

## HEPA Filter Grades Explained: H10 Through H14

The EN 1822 standard provides a clear classification system. Understanding where each grade falls helps you evaluate any air purifier claim:

### EPA Group (Efficient Particulate Air) — H10 to H12

These are lower-efficiency filters that do not meet the True HEPA threshold. They are legitimate products for applications like basic HVAC filtration, but they are not appropriate for air purifiers marketed for allergy or asthma relief.

**H10:** ≥85% efficiency at MPPS. Captures most large particles (pollen, large dust) but allows a significant percentage of smaller allergens through. Used in basic building ventilation.

**H11:** ≥95% efficiency at MPPS. Better than H10, but still allows 5% of the most penetrating particles through — 167 times more leakage than True HEPA. Used in commercial HVAC systems where high efficiency is desired but True HEPA is not required.

**H12:** ≥99.5% efficiency at MPPS. Approaching True HEPA performance but not quite there. Allows 0.5% of particles through — 10 times more than H13. Used in pharmaceutical pre-filtration and some industrial cleanrooms.

### HEPA Group (High Efficiency Particulate Air) — H13 to H14

These are the grades that qualify as True HEPA. They represent the performance threshold that matters for respiratory health applications.

**H13:** ≥99.95% efficiency at MPPS. The standard for residential True HEPA air purifiers and hospital patient rooms. Captures virtually all allergens, mold spores, bacteria, and fine particulate matter. This is the grade you want for home air purification.

**H14:** ≥99.995% efficiency at MPPS. Used in operating rooms, pharmaceutical manufacturing, and semiconductor fabrication. Ten times more efficient than H13 at the most penetrating size. Overkill for residential use but available in premium purifiers like the IQAir HealthPro Plus (which uses a proprietary HyperHEPA exceeding H14 performance).

### ULPA Group (Ultra Low Penetration Air) — U15 to U17

Beyond HEPA, ULPA filters capture 99.9995% to 99.999995% of particles. These are used exclusively in semiconductor cleanrooms, nanotechnology labs, and BSL-4 biocontainment facilities. They are not available in consumer air purifiers and are included here only for completeness.

## The Math: Why a Few Percent Matters

The difference between 90% and 99.97% sounds small. It is not. Here is why:

### Single-Pass Efficiency

On a single pass through the filter, a 90%-efficient filter lets 10% of particles through. A 99.97%-efficient filter lets 0.03% through. That is a 333x difference in leakage per pass.

But air purifiers do not filter air once — they filter it continuously. The air in a room cycles through the purifier multiple times per hour (measured as ACH). This is where the efficiency gap compounds.

### Cumulative Effect Over Multiple Air Changes

After multiple air changes, the steady-state particle concentration in the room depends on the filter efficiency:

| Air Changes | 90% Filter (HEPA-type) | 99.97% Filter (True HEPA) |
|:-----------:|:---------------------:|:------------------------:|
| 1 ACH | 10% of particles remain | 0.03% remain |
| 2 ACH | 1% remain | 0.0009% remain |
| 4 ACH | 0.01% remain | Effectively zero |
| 6 ACH | 0.0001% remain | Effectively zero |

Both filters eventually reduce concentrations to very low levels with enough air changes. But the True HEPA filter reaches near-zero concentrations 2–3 air changes faster. In a bedroom running at 4 ACH, the True HEPA room is essentially particle-free after one hour. The HEPA-type room takes nearly two hours to reach comparable levels.

### Why Speed Matters for Health

Allergens and irritants become airborne continuously — from movement, HVAC cycling, pets walking through, doors opening. After each re-aerosolization event, the purifier must bring concentrations back down. A True HEPA filter recovers to clean-air baseline faster because it captures more particles per pass. A HEPA-type filter takes longer, meaning you breathe elevated concentrations for more minutes per event.

Over an 8-hour sleep period with multiple disturbance events (rolling over, pet entering, HVAC cycling), the cumulative exposure difference is meaningful for respiratory-sensitive individuals.

### The Practical Threshold

For general air quality improvement in a home without specific respiratory concerns, a HEPA-type filter provides noticeable improvement over no filtration at all. You will see less dust settling on surfaces and the air will feel fresher.

For allergy management, asthma control, mold spore reduction, or nursery air quality, True HEPA is the minimum responsible recommendation. The performance gap is real, measurable, and compounds with continuous use.

## How to Verify HEPA Claims Before You Buy

Manufacturers can label products however they want — there is no pre-market filtration labeling requirement for consumer air purifiers in the US. Here is how to verify claims independently:

### 1. Check AHAM Verifide Certification

The Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers (AHAM) runs the Verifide program, which independently tests air purifiers and publishes certified CADR data. If a purifier is AHAM Verifide certified, its performance claims have been independently verified.

Search the AHAM directory at [aham.org](https://www.aham.org/) for any model you are considering. If it appears with published CADR data, the filtration claims are credible. If it does not appear, the manufacturer has not submitted to independent testing — which does not necessarily mean the claims are false, but it means they are unverified.

**Notable brands that do not participate in AHAM:** Dyson, IQAir, and some premium brands do not submit to AHAM testing. Their claims are based on manufacturer testing. This does not mean the claims are inaccurate, but independent verification is absent.

### 2. Look for Specific Efficiency Claims

A legitimate True HEPA filter states its efficiency precisely:
- ✅ "99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns" (US DOE standard)
- ✅ "H13 HEPA: 99.95% at MPPS per EN 1822" (European standard)
- ✅ "99.995% at MPPS" (H14 grade)

Red flag claims:
- ❌ "Captures up to 99% of particles" (what size? what test method?)
- ❌ "HEPA-grade filtration" (what grade specifically?)
- ❌ "Removes 99% of airborne allergens" (tested how?)

The word "up to" before any percentage is almost always a signal that the actual performance is lower. "Up to 99%" could mean 85% on average.

### 3. Check the Filter Itself

On reputable brands, the filter cartridge or its packaging states the EN 1822 grade (H13, H14) or the US HEPA standard. If neither the product listing, the manual, nor the filter itself specifies a grade or efficiency standard, assume HEPA-type.

### 4. Check CARB Certification

While CARB (California Air Resources Board) certification is about ozone emissions rather than filtration efficiency, CARB-certified purifiers tend to be from reputable brands that also accurately represent their filtration capabilities. CARB certification is a secondary trust signal.

## Common Marketing Tricks to Watch For

### "99.99% Filtration"

Some purifiers claim "99.99% filtration" without specifying particle size. HEPA-type filters can capture 99.99% of particles at 5 microns (pollen-sized) while capturing only 85% at 0.3 microns. The impressive number is technically accurate but misleading — it describes performance at the easiest particle size, not the hardest.

True HEPA specifies the particle size because the test is conducted at the hardest size to capture. Any claim without a particle size specification is likely cherry-picking favorable test conditions.

### "Equivalent to HEPA"

"Equivalent" is not "certified." A filter may perform similarly to HEPA in ideal conditions but degrade faster, perform inconsistently, or fail to meet the standard across the full range of particle sizes. Without independent testing, "equivalent" is unverifiable.

### "Medical-Grade" or "Hospital-Grade"

These terms have no regulatory definition. They imply H13 or H14 performance but carry no enforceable standard. Some purifiers using these labels are genuinely H13/H14; others use the terms to elevate HEPA-type or H12 filters. Always check for the specific EN 1822 grade or US DOE HEPA standard behind the marketing label.

### CADR Without Context

A HEPA-type purifier can have high CADR because its lower-resistance filter allows more airflow. High CADR with a HEPA-type filter means you are pushing more air through a less effective filter — moving particles around without capturing them as efficiently. CADR is only meaningful when paired with confirmed filter efficiency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is HEPA-type good enough for allergies and asthma?
For serious allergy or asthma management, no. HEPA-type filters capture 85–95% of particles at best, meaning 5–15% of allergens pass through on every air cycle. Over hours of continuous operation, this significantly reduces the purifier's ability to maintain low allergen concentrations. True HEPA (99.97%) or H13 HEPA (99.95%) is the minimum standard recommended by the EPA, the Asthma and Allergy Foundation of America, and most allergists.

### What filter grade is True HEPA?
True HEPA corresponds to H13 or H14 under the European EN 1822 classification. The US DOE defines True HEPA as capturing 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns, which aligns with the H13 grade (99.95% at MPPS) and H14 grade (99.995% at MPPS). Filters below H13 — including H10, H11, and H12 — do not qualify as True HEPA regardless of marketing language.

### What is the difference between H13 and H14 HEPA filters?
H13 captures 99.95% at MPPS. H14 captures 99.995% — ten times fewer particles escape. Both are excellent for residential use. H14 is standard in hospital operating rooms and pharmaceutical cleanrooms. For home air purification, H13 is more than sufficient. The price premium for H14 residential purifiers is rarely justified unless you have severe respiratory conditions or are immunocompromised.

### How can I verify if a filter is really True HEPA?
Three verification steps: (1) Check AHAM Verifide certification at aham.org for independently tested CADR data. (2) Look for specific efficiency claims — True HEPA states 99.97% at 0.3 microns or 99.95% at MPPS. Vague claims like "captures up to 99%" without a particle size are red flags. (3) Check for EN 1822 grade labeling (H13, H14) on the filter or in product documentation.

### Why do manufacturers sell HEPA-type if True HEPA is better?
Cost. True HEPA filter media is significantly more expensive to manufacture. The dense fiber structure costs more in raw materials and requires tighter quality control. HEPA-type filters use looser fiber media that requires a smaller, cheaper fan motor. The result is a lower retail price that attracts budget shoppers who may not understand the performance difference.

### Does HEPA filter grade matter for dust and pet dander?
Yes. Dust (0.5–100 µm) and pet dander (2.5–10 µm) are larger than the 0.3-micron test threshold, so even HEPA-type captures a high percentage per pass. But the difference compounds over 24/7 operation: True HEPA maintaining 99.97% keeps room concentrations significantly lower than HEPA-type at 90%. For households with pets or dust sensitivity, True HEPA provides measurably cleaner air.

### Is H13 HEPA the same as True HEPA?
Functionally equivalent but defined by different standards. True HEPA is a US term (DOE): 99.97% at 0.3 microns. H13 is European (EN 1822): 99.95% at MPPS. The MPPS is typically 0.1–0.2 microns — actually a harder test. An H13-rated filter meets or exceeds True HEPA performance. Both indicate genuine high-efficiency filtration.

### Can a HEPA-type filter be upgraded to True HEPA?
Usually not. True HEPA filters have higher airflow resistance. A purifier designed for HEPA-type has a fan motor sized for lower resistance. Inserting a True HEPA filter would restrict airflow, reduce CADR, increase noise, and risk overheating the motor. Switching from HEPA-type to True HEPA typically requires buying a new purifier designed for the denser filter media.
